02 / 10 Problem

Standard LTV models
break in travel

Most LTV frameworks assume that once a customer stops purchasing, there is some probability they have permanently churned.

This is valid for subscriptions. For travel eSIM, it is wrong.

Core thesis A customer silent for 9 months is not lost — they are waiting for their next trip. Their behaviour follows annual macro-cycles, not random dropout.
80%
of customers are "leisure once" — buy once, then dormant 6–12 months before next trip
~365d
median inter-trip interval for leisure segment — driven by annual travel cycles
55%
of users have zero revenue in any given 12-month label window — but most will return

04 / 10 Hito 1 · Key Finding

Kaplan-Meier proof:
the 12-month plateau

What the plateau means S(t) stops declining sharply at ~12 months. A significant fraction of users are not lost — they are waiting for their next trip.
~50%
of users still within their travel cycle at 12 months (have not yet returned, but will)
68%
repeat purchase rate for users with active retention pings — vs 0% for users with no device activity

The KM estimator handles right-censoring correctly: users who haven't yet returned are censored at the observation date — not treated as churned.

05 / 10 Hito 2 · Model A

BG/NBD + Gamma-Gamma:
principled baseline

The BG/NBD model was designed exactly for non-contractual settings — it estimates the probability of being "alive" combined with expected purchase frequency, with no renewal signal required.

We fit it as the principled probabilistic baseline. The Gamma-Gamma component models expected spend per transaction (conditional on activity).

The key assumption — and where it fails BG/NBD assumes a constant geometric dropout rate. Travel users don't drop out randomly — they follow annual macro-cycles. The model reads the 12-month plateau as high churn probability.

06 / 10 Hito 2 · Model B

LightGBM with
survival-informed features

Gradient-boosted trees on an RFM + survival feature matrix. Tweedie loss (power=1.5) handles the zero-inflated, heavy-tailed label distribution natively.

The key differentiator: survival features that encode the annual macro-cycle structure BG/NBD cannot use.

09 / 10 Hito 3 · Retention

Retention discount:
dormant ≠ lost

1,084 users are dormant (recency >180 days, ≥1 prior purchase). The question is not whether to re-engage them — it's how much it's worth spending to do so.

Break-even condition: max_discount = P(reactivate) × predicted_margin

This is a testable hypothesis Offer €0, €3, €5, €8 discounts to randomly-assigned dormant cohorts. Measure reactivation rates. One A/B test directly calibrates the P(reactivate | discount) curve — and validates or refines every number on this slide.
